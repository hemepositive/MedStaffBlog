<div class="key-message">

VTE risk in GI surgery ranges dramatically — from &lt;0.5% in laparoscopic cholecystectomy to 10% in emergency open colorectal procedures. Effective prophylaxis requires risk stratification, procedure-specific knowledge, and individualized planning.

</div>

## Quick risk assessment: which procedures matter most?

| Procedure type | Typical VTE risk | Prophylaxis approach |
| -------------- | ---------------- | -------------------- |
| 🟢 Laparoscopic cholecystectomy / hernia | <0.5% | Early ambulation only |
| 🟡 Elective colorectal cancer resection | 1–3% | Mechanical ± pharmacologic |
| 🟠 Open/emergency colorectal, IBD | 6–10% | Pharmacologic + mechanical |
| 🟠 Major HPB (pancreas, liver) | 2–7% | Pharmacologic + mechanical |
| 🟡 Gastric cancer gastrectomy | 1–2% | Mechanical ± pharmacologic |

*Data source: Lavikainen et al. (2023) — meta-analysis of 8M+ patients, 115 procedure types.*

## Risk stratification framework

All patients need a formal VTE risk assessment — use the [Caprini Score calculator](https://www.mdcalc.com/calc/3970/caprini-score-venous-thromboembolism-2005) or institutional equivalent to guide prophylaxis planning.

- **Very low risk (Caprini 0; <0.5%):** early ambulation alone — no pharmacologic or mechanical prophylaxis recommended
- **Low risk (Caprini 1–2; ~1.5%):** mechanical prophylaxis (IPC)
- **Moderate risk (Caprini 3–4; ~3%):** LMWH, LDUH, **or** mechanical prophylaxis (IPC) — equivalent alternatives at this risk level
- **High risk (Caprini ≥5; ≥6%; cancer, IBD, emergency, open):** pharmacologic (LMWH or LDUH) **plus** mechanical (IPC)

Key patient factors: age, BMI, history of VTE, malignancy, hypercoagulable states, and pelvic/abdominal surgical complexity.

## Pharmacologic options

### First-line: low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH)

- **Enoxaparin 40 mg SC daily** — preferred: once-daily dosing, lower HIT risk
- **Obesity (BMI ≥40 kg/m²):** consider 40 mg SC every 12 hours **or** 0.5 mg/kg SC daily (limited data, derived primarily from non-oncology populations)

### Renal impairment (CrCl <30 mL/min)

- **Enoxaparin 30 mg SC once daily** (FDA-approved dose reduction for abdominal surgery prophylaxis)
- **Alternative:** UFH 5,000 units SC every 8 hours (preferred when enoxaparin is not suitable)

## Timing of prophylaxis initiation

### Cancer surgery: preoperative initiation

ITAC 2019/2022 guidelines and NCCN recommend starting LMWH **2–12 hours preoperatively**, continuing postoperatively for at least 7–10 days. Example regimen: enoxaparin 40 mg SC 2–12 hours preoperatively, then once daily postoperatively.

### Non-cancer GI surgery: postoperative initiation

Recent evidence favors **postoperative initiation** to minimize bleeding risk. A 2023 meta-analysis (Klonis et al., 24,922 patients) found that initiating chemoprophylaxis before skin closure increased all bleeding (RR 1.56), major bleeding (RR 1.63), transfusion (RR 1.48), and reintervention (RR 1.94) versus postoperative initiation — without reducing VTE rates.

**General principle: start 12–24 hours after surgery, once hemostasis is established.** With high bleeding risk or major intraoperative hemorrhage, delay to postoperative day 1; UFH may be preferred for its shorter half-life and reversibility. Continue mechanical prophylaxis (IPC) regardless.

## Mechanical prophylaxis (IPC)

- Apply **before anesthesia induction**; wear ≥18–20 hours/day to be effective
- Appropriate as monotherapy for Caprini 0–2 or when bleeding risk is high
- For Caprini ≥5, always **combine** with pharmacologic prophylaxis

## Special populations

- **GI malignancy:** consider **extended prophylaxis — 28 days total** postoperative (enoxaparin 40 mg daily, dalteparin 5,000 units daily, or eligible DOAC per NCCN/ASCO) for high-risk patients without high bleeding risk
- **IBD:** treat as high-risk, especially emergency/open procedures (VTE up to 9%); consider extended prophylaxis
- **Hepatectomy:** pooled VTE 5.3% with mechanical alone vs 2.5% with pharmacologic added — combined approach strongly supported
- **Minimally invasive surgery:** laparoscopy does not eliminate VTE risk (pneumoperitoneum causes venous stasis); follow Caprini-based prophylaxis regardless of approach

## Practical implementation checklist

**Preoperative**

- Calculate the Caprini Score (0 = very low, 1–2 = low, 3–4 = moderate, ≥5 = high)
- Document bleeding risk (coagulopathy, anticoagulation, recent surgery)
- Decide: pharmacologic + mechanical, pharmacologic alone, or mechanical alone

**Perioperative**

- Place IPC before anesthesia induction
- Cancer surgery: start LMWH 2–12 hours preoperatively
- Non-cancer surgery: start LMWH/UFH 12–24 hours postoperatively once hemostasis is established

**Discharge planning**

- High-risk GI cancer patients: arrange supplies for the 28-day extended course; provide injection teaching or prescribe an eligible DOAC
- Document the extended prophylaxis plan in the discharge summary

<div class="takeaway">

**Tailor, don't assume.** Assess procedure-specific VTE and bleeding risk with the Caprini score, combine mechanical and pharmacologic prophylaxis for high-risk patients (Caprini ≥5), and reserve extended prophylaxis for truly high-risk populations rather than applying it as a blanket policy. Distinguish cancer from non-cancer surgery when deciding timing — preoperative initiation for cancer surgery, postoperative for non-cancer abdominal surgery.
